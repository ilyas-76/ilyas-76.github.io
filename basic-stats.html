📐 Concept 01

Basic Statistics

The language of data. Learn to describe, summarize, and understand any dataset using a handful of powerful numbers.

What is it?

Imagine you scored 78, 82, 90, 70, and 85 on five tests. You could list all five scores — but your teacher wants one number that captures "how you're doing overall." Basic statistics gives us exactly those summary numbers.

Every dataset has a shape, a center, and a spread. Basic statistics is simply the toolkit for measuring all three.

Central Tendency — "Where is the middle?"

Mean (Average)

Add everything up, divide by how many there are. The most familiar summary statistic.

Formula
x̄ = (x₁ + x₂ + … + xₙ) / n
x̄ = sample mean  |  n = number of values
Real-World Example

Test scores: 70, 78, 82, 85, 90
Mean = (70+78+82+85+90) / 5 = 405 / 5 = 81
Your average score is 81.

Watch Out

A billionaire walking into a room raises the average salary of everyone there — even though nobody actually got richer. The mean is sensitive to extreme values (outliers).

Median

Sort all values, pick the one in the middle. If there's an even count, average the two middle values.

Formula (odd n)
Median = x₍ₙ₊₁₎/₂
The value at position (n+1)/2 when data is sorted
Real-World Example

House prices in a street: $200K, $220K, $230K, $240K, $1.5M
Sorted middle = $230K. That's more representative than the mean of $478K, which the mansion inflates.

Mode

The value that appears most often. A dataset can have one mode, many modes, or none at all.

Real-World Example

Shoe sizes sold today: 7, 8, 8, 9, 10, 8, 7, 11, 8
Mode = 8 — the store should stock more size 8 shoes!

Dispersion — "How spread out is it?"

Range

The simplest spread measure: highest minus lowest.

Formula
Range = x_max − x_min

Variance

Measures how far each value is from the mean, on average — squaring the differences so they don't cancel out.

Population Variance
σ² = Σ(xᵢ − μ)² / N
μ = population mean  |  N = population size
Sample Variance (Bessel's correction)
s² = Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / (n − 1)
We use n−1 (not n) to avoid underestimating variability from a sample

Standard Deviation (SD)

The square root of variance — putting the number back in the original units so it's human-readable.

Formula
σ = √σ²
Real-World Example

Two factories both produce bolts averaging 10 mm. Factory A has SD = 0.1 mm, Factory B has SD = 2 mm. Factory A is far more consistent — even though they have the same mean. SD reveals the consistency story the mean hides.

Coefficient of Variation (CV)

Standardises the SD as a percentage of the mean, letting you compare variability across datasets with different units or scales.

Formula
CV = (σ / μ) × 100%
Shape — "What does the distribution look like?"
⚖️

Skewness

Measures symmetry. Positive skew = long tail to the right (e.g. income). Negative skew = long tail to the left.

⛰️

Kurtosis

Measures the "peakedness." High kurtosis = sharp peak, heavy tails. Low kurtosis = flat, thin tails.

📦

Quartiles (IQR)

Split data into four equal parts. The interquartile range (Q3−Q1) is the spread of the middle 50%.
